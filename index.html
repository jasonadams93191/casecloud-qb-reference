CaseCloud ↔ QuickBooks Online

How your matter-management system and your accounting connect — what moves, what stays put, and how settlements, costs, and write-offs are handled. A plain-English guide for your finance team.

The big picture

Your data lives in two systems joined by a middleware called DBSync, installed as a QuickBooks private app. One line captures the whole model:

CaseCloud decides. QuickBooks records.

  • CaseCloud (Salesforce) is the system of record for matters, expenses, trust ledgers, and fees — you decide there what is owed and to whom.
  • QuickBooks Online is the system of record for cash — what was actually paid out and received.
  • The middleware moves records both ways: a PUSH creates checks and deposits in QuickBooks; a WRITE-BACK returns the printed check number to CaseCloud to close the loop.

Three things drive everything

  1. CaseCloud drives the action. If it isn’t right in CaseCloud first, it won’t be right in QuickBooks. QuickBooks is the destination, never the starting point.
  2. “Request” is the trigger. Nothing flows automatically. A transaction must be Requested (correct status + a Method selected) before the middleware picks it up.
  3. QuickBooks writes back. Once the check prints, the check number returns to the CaseCloud transaction — typically within 10–15 minutes.

QuickBooks setup — the two-account model

Legal accounting requires the firm’s money and client money to stay strictly separate. In QuickBooks that means two bank accounts — Operating and Trust — plus a Trust Liability account so client funds never appear as firm income.

1 · Operating account

The firm’s money — earned fees and costs the firm advances on a client’s behalf.

  • ✓ Firm pays case costs from here (Step 1 of the cost lifecycle).
  • ✓ Earned attorney fees land here as income.
  • ✓ Receives reimbursements from Trust.

2 · Trust (IOLTA) account

The client’s money — settlement funds held on behalf of clients until disbursed.

  • ✓ Settlement deposits land here.
  • ✓ Liens, providers, and the client are paid from here.
  • ✓ Reimburses Operating for advanced costs.

The QuickBooks accounts we create

Why the Trust Liability account matters

Client money is a liability, not revenue. Pairing the Trust bank with a Trust Liability account keeps the books compliant: the two always move together and net to zero for the firm. The integration posts to both automatically — deposits raise the liability, disbursements lower it.

What syncs — and what deliberately does not

Just as important as what moves is what stays put. Some records are intentionally kept out of QuickBooks so your books stay clean.

Cost lifecycle — paid by operating, reimbursed by trust

When the firm advances a case cost, there are two separate cash movements and two QuickBooks records — that is the part people miss. The expense’s status tracks both.

Pending→ Approved→ Requested→ Paid→ Reimbursed

Step 1 — Firm advances the cost

Expense Requested with Disbursement Account = Operating, Method = Check.

QuickBooks: a check from the Operating bank account to the vendor.

Result: check number writes back; status → Paid (the firm paid it from operating).

Step 2 — Trust reimburses the firm

A reimbursement transaction with Disbursement Account = Trust, once settlement funds are in trust.

QuickBooks: a check/transfer from the Trust account back to Operating.

Result: “Amount Reimbursed from Trust” fills; status auto-flips Paid → Reimbursed.

The #1 failure to avoid

Someone jumps Approved → Paid by hand. That shortcut skips the sync entirely: no QB check, the status never flips to Reimbursed, and the reimbursed amount is wrong. Let the flow drive the status — never set “Paid” manually unless you are recording a check that was truly cut outside the system.

Write-offs

A write-off adjusts the expense in CaseCloud only — it never creates a QuickBooks check. That is the whole purpose of the Non-Ledger path.

The rules that keep it clean

  • ✓ A full write-off generates a disbursement with Disbursement Account = Non-Ledger so the middleware skips it. Build the flow to set Non-Ledger explicitly and validate it before status hits Requested.
  • ✓ Reimburse first, then write off the remainder. The partial-write-off math only balances in that order.
  • ✓ “Amount Paid After Reimbursement” intentionally includes the write-off — that is the formula by design, not double-counting.
  • ✓ Give the team Write-Off and Reverse Write-Off quick actions on the matter so nobody hand-edits the fields.

Watch for the “Operating instead of Non-Ledger” bug

The classic failure is a full write-off that stamps Operating on the disbursement instead of Non-Ledger — it then tries to push a real check to QuickBooks. If you catch it before status moves to Requested, change the account to Non-Ledger. If a QB check already generated, void it and call ACS.

Automating settlement distribution

When a settlement arrives, CaseCloud distributes the gross recovery in a fixed order — the waterfall. The Settlement record is the trigger; a “Distribute Settlement” action generates the child disbursements, each pre-set with a Method and Disbursement Account and left in Requested status for the middleware to push.

The waterfall, in order

  1. Attorney fee — auto-calculated from the matter’s advpm__Fee_Arrangement__c contingency % → drawn from Trust, recognized as income.
  2. Reimburse firm-advanced costs — the operating→trust cycle, for every expense still at “Paid.”
  3. Pay liens / providers / experts — checks from Trust to each advpm__Participant__c payee.
  4. Net to client — check from Trust to the client.
  5. Write off any uncollectable remainder — after reimbursement, using the Non-Ledger path.

Guardrails the automation must enforce

  • Trust math pulls from Amount Received (the actual deposit), never the gross settlement face value.
  • Every generated transaction gets a Method populated, or it silently fails to sync.
  • Validate that total distributions equal Amount Received, so trust is never over-drawn.
  • One-to-one matching — the model can’t split a transaction into part-applied / part-credited. If an amount changes, void cleanly and reissue rather than splitting.

Native CaseCloud does the heavy lifting for the ledger (advpm__Trust__c / advpm__Trust_Transaction__c) and the fee calc (advpm__Fee_Arrangement__c); the waterfall sequencing and the QB push are custom flow plus DBSync.

The rules that keep it clean

If everyone follows these, the large majority of sync problems never happen.

Do

  • ✓ Always populate Method before saving. No Method = no sync — the single most common silent failure.
  • ✓ Create fee and referral transactions from the correct subtab, not the parent tab.
  • ✓ Route every deposit through a Settlement record — the trust ledger keys off it.
  • ✓ Reimburse first, then write off the remaining balance.
  • ✓ Set the right Disbursement Account (Operating / Trust / Non-Ledger) every time.

Don’t

  • ✗ Don’t type checks directly into QuickBooks for matter disbursements — they can’t write back.
  • ✗ Don’t jump a transaction Approved → Paid manually — it bypasses the whole sync.
  • ✗ Don’t manually edit Integration Status — automation drives it; edits corrupt the trust roll-up.
  • ✗ Don’t delete a QB record that came from CaseCloud without marking CaseCloud Paid first — it re-creates.
  • ✗ Don’t re-use or duplicate vendor records — flag suspected duplicates.
